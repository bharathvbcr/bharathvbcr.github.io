    Assessing Cognitive Performance During Dynamic Activities with Varying Insole Textures

      ‚Ä¢ Investigated the effects of textured insoles on cognitive performance during dynamic activities, specifically focusing on divided attention scenarios.

      ‚Ä¢ Hypothesized that enhanced somatosensory feedback from textured insoles would improve cognitive resource allocation, particularly in situations requiring divided attention.

      ‚Ä¢ Utilized a dual-task paradigm with an auditory Stroop test and dynamic physical activities to understand how textured insoles influence cognitive performance.

      ‚Ä¢ This research may reveal strategies for enhancing performance and reducing injury risk in athletes and inform interventions for improving balance and cognitive function in older adults and individuals with sensory deficits.

    Situation

      ‚Ä¢ The intricate interplay between sensory feedback, motor control, and cognitive processing has long fascinated researchers in the fields of neuroscience, psychology, and biomechanics.

      ‚Ä¢ Recent advancements in our understanding of these complex relationships have opened new avenues for investigation, particularly in the realm of how peripheral sensory inputs can influence higher-order cognitive functions.

      ‚Ä¢ Of particular interest is the potential impact of foot perturbation on cognitive processes, a finding that has sparked a series of studies exploring novel ways to optimize cognitive performance during dynamic activities.

    Task

      ‚Ä¢ Delve deeper into this fascinating area by examining the effects of textured insoles on cognitive function, with a specific focus on situations requiring divided attention, such as those encountered in dual-task paradigms.

    Action

      ‚Ä¢ Recruited fourteen subjects (14 males, aged 19 to 28 years old) for this study.

      ‚Ä¢ Each subject participated in approximately 30 minutes of testing and data collection at the lab.

      ‚Ä¢ Subjects' cognitive abilities were evaluated during testing to determine the effects of four distinct insole treatments.

      ‚Ä¢ All methodologies and procedures employed throughout this study were carried out with the approval of the Arizona State University Institutional Review Board.

    Methodology

    Visual Analog Scale Test (VAST): Participants rated the perceived roughness of four insole treatments (their own insole and three textured insoles) using a Visual Analog Scale (VAS) ranging from 0 (least rough) to 10 (most rough).

    Dynamic Auditory Stroop Test (DAST): Participants performed an auditory Stroop test while running on a track, wearing each of the four insole treatments in a randomized order. The test involved responding verbally to auditory cues ("Right" or "Left") presented through a headset, indicating whether the word matched the ear it was played in (congruent) or not (incongruent).

    Problems Faced:

    VAST:

    Ensuring consistent and accurate participant responses for perceived roughness. This was addressed by providing clear instructions and allowing participants to adjust their ratings until satisfied with their perceptual ordering.

    DAST:

    a. High variability in individual response latencies. This was addressed by analyzing data for each participant separately to compare textured insole conditions to the control condition.

    b. Outliers in response latencies due to participants inhaling or gasping for air during the running task. This was addressed by filtering out response latencies outside the typical range of 0.75 to 1.25 seconds.

    Result

      ‚Ä¢ Textured insoles, which enhance sensory feedback from the feet, influence cognitive performance during dynamic activities.

      ‚Ä¢ This enhanced feedback will lead to more efficient cognitive resource allocation, particularly in situations requiring divided attention.

      ‚Ä¢ Focusing on a dynamically recruited sample provided valuable insights into the effects of textured insoles during fast-paced activities.

      ‚Ä¢ By understanding this relationship, we may uncover strategies for enhancing athletic performance and reducing injury risk.

    Conclusion

      ‚Ä¢ The study clarified the complex interactions between sensory feedback, motor control, and cognitive processing in the context of textured insoles and dynamic activities.
